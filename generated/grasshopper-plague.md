# Grasshopper Plague

The Grasshopper Plague of 1873-1877 was a devastating agricultural disaster that swept across the Great Plains and Midwest regions of the United States. The event began in July 1874 when massive swarms of Rocky Mountain locusts (Melanoplus spretus) descended upon farms from the Dakotas to Texas, with particularly severe impact in Minnesota, Iowa, Nebraska, Kansas, and Missouri. The swarms were so dense they reportedly blocked out the sun, with estimates suggesting individual swarms contained trillions of insects and covered areas up to 1,800 miles long and 110 miles wide. The locusts consumed everything in their path—crops, grass, tree bark, wool from living sheep, and even wooden tool handles—leaving complete devastation in their wake.

The economic and social impact of the plague was catastrophic for frontier settlers who had recently claimed homesteads under the Homestead Act. Entire seasons of wheat, corn, and other crops were destroyed within hours of the swarms' arrival, leaving families without food stores for winter or seed for the following spring. Many homesteaders abandoned their claims and returned East, while those who remained faced starvation and depended on aid from eastern states and the federal government. The crisis prompted one of the first major federal disaster relief efforts in American history, with Congress appropriating funds and military rations for affected settlers. Communities organized mutual aid networks, and churches coordinated relief supplies to help families survive until they could replant.

The plague finally subsided after 1877, with the last major swarm occurring in 1877. The Rocky Mountain locust, which had been one of the most numerous insects in North America, mysteriously went extinct by the early 20th century—the only pest species known to have been driven to extinction. Modern theories suggest that agricultural development destroyed critical breeding grounds in river valleys of the Rocky Mountains. For those who survived the plague years, like many Iowa and Nebraska homesteaders, the event became a defining moment of resilience and community cooperation in the settlement of the American frontier.
